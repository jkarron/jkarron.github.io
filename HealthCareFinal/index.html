Maryland's Increase in Stage 1 Breast Cancer Rates

by Julia Karron

An increase in stage 1 breast cancer rates means catching deadly cancers earlier and saving more women's lives. This story -- told through graphics -- documents how the Affordable Care Act's preventive service mandate helped women.

As Technology Improved, Diagnosis Rates Increased

When mammography became the industry standard for indentifying breast cancer in the 80's the number of identified invasive breast cancers increased.

But this wasn't because more women had cancer: it was because better technology meant doctors could catch more cancers, the Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results Program said.

The Mortality Rate Due to Breast Cancer in Maryland Fluctuated per County

When cancers are caught at earlier stages, the likelihood of saving someones life increases.

This increase should cause mortality rates to drop.

In most Maryland counties, there was a decrease in the mortality rate from breast cancer after the Affordable Care Act was implemented.

Some saw an increase in mortality rates.

The Number of Stage 1 Diagnoses in Maryland Increased After Obamacare Started

With an almost 4 percentage point jump in stage 1 identified breast cancers, Maryland women appear to be getting mammograms when their cancers are treatable.

This chart shows the increase between the number of stage 1 identified breast cancers from 2006-2009 and 2011-2014.

Maryland's Black Women Also Had Increased Stage 1 Diagnoses

Diagnoses of stage 1 breast cancer among black women increased three percentage points.

According to Dr. Regina Hampton, black women's stage 1 breast cancers tend to be more aggressive, so catching them early is key.

This trend towards earlier screenigns could produce a drop in the mortality rate of black women.

Those Without Insurance Suffer

Under the ACA, there is no out-of-pocket cost for a mammogram because it falls under the preventive service mandate.

But if a woman lacks insurance, she is twice as likely to have not gotten a mammogram in the past two years.

For women who need access to mammograhy screenings, having insurance eliminates the financial barrier.
